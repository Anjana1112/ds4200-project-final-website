Mental Health Care Disparities

Introduction to Topic

Our project focuses on analyzing mental health service availability and disparities across the United States using data from the National Mental Health Services Survey (N-MHSS). This topic is critical as mental health care access remains inconsistent across different regions, demographic groups, and facility types, often leaving vulnerable populations underserved. By exploring factors such as geographic location, facility type, patient demographics, and the use of telemedicine, we aim to uncover patterns and disparities in mental health service provision. Understanding these trends is vital for policymakers and health care providers to address gaps in service, improve care equity, and ensure that individuals with mental health needs receive appropriate and timely treatment.

References

Gao et al. (2024) highlight a global mental health treatment gap, with only 42-44% seeking care, especially in low- and middle-income nations. Access is influenced by factors like age, gender, ethnicity, and income, while barriers include cost, transportation, and stigma. Cantor et al. (2024) found that 80% of U.S. mental health facilities offer telehealth, with services varying by facility type and geography. Urban and private hospitals are more likely to provide telehealth, but access is uneven, with median wait times of 14 days. Mirza et al. (2020) explored language barriers in mental health care for patients with limited English proficiency, finding challenges in communication and a lack of qualified interpreters. The study emphasizes the need for tailored training to improve care. These papers inform our research on regional disparities, demographic factors, and the growing role of telemedicine, providing essential context for visualizing inequalities in mental health services.

Introduction to Data

The data being used is from the National Mental Health Services Survey, which consolidates data from every known mental health treatment facility in the United States from 2010 to 2020. The types of mental health treatment facilities included in the dataset are as follows: psychiatric hospitals, general hospitals, Veterans Affairs medical centers, partial hospitalization/day treatment mental health facilities, outpatient mental health facilities, residential treatment centers for children, residential treatment centers for adults, multi-setting mental health facilities, community mental health centers, non-psychiatric hospitals, and other (any facilities that don’t fall into the previously named categories). The topics included in the survey include facility type, operation, and primary treatment focus, facility treatment characteristics (e.g., settings of care; mental health treatment modalities, pharmacotherapies; supportive services and practices; services provided to clients with co occurring mental health and substance use disorders; dedicated or exclusively designed programs or groups offered; seclusion or restraint practices; and crisis intervention team availability), facility operating characteristics (e.g., age groups accepted; availability of treatment services provided in non-English languages and in sign language for the deaf and hard-of-hearing; and smoking policy), facility management characteristics (e.g., quality improvement practices; use of electronic resources to accomplish work activities; licensure, certification, and accreditation; sources of payment and funding), and client characteristics. The data was collected through secure web-based questionnaires, paper questionnaires sent by mail, and computer-assisted telephone interviews. In 2020, 14,187 total eligible facilities were surveyed. The dataset is accessible in formats for SAS, SPSS, Stata, Delimited, and R, and can be downloaded through the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) website.

Therapy Services vs Facility Types

This radar chart compares the availability of various therapy services across different facility types. The key takeaways from this visualization are:

Telemedicine: Telemedicine services are highly available at Veterans Administration facilities, which is logical given that many veterans do not reside in these facilities but instead rely on remote access to support when needed. This highlights the facility’s adaptability to meet veterans’ needs through virtual care options.

Language Services: Inpatient facilities and community-based centers excel in providing language services, reflecting a strong commitment to inclusivity by offering care in languages other than English. Unfortunately, residential facilities fall significantly behind in this area, suggesting a lack of accessibility for non-English-speaking individuals in long-term care settings.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Group Therapy: These therapy types are among the most widely available services across all facility types, with their lines consistently near the outer edge of the chart. This indicates their role as foundational therapeutic approaches in mental health treatment.

Trauma Therapy: While trauma therapy is broadly available across most facility types, its provision is notably limited in residential and inpatient facilities. This raises potential concerns, as individuals in these settings may benefit significantly from trauma-informed care.

Activity Therapy: Residential and inpatient facilities are leaders in offering activity therapy, with inpatient facilities providing the highest levels. This emphasis aligns with the nature of these settings, where individuals, often residing for extended periods, have greater opportunities to engage in structured activities. In contrast, activity therapy availability in most other facilities is relatively low, often below 15%, except for multi-setting facilities.

Overall Trends: The chart underscores the strong presence of core therapies, such as cognitive behavioral and group therapies, across most facility types, while highlighting service gaps in areas like telemedicine, trauma therapy, and language services in specific settings. Residential facilities, in particular, appear to struggle with offering diverse therapy services, especially in language, telemedicine, and trauma therapy.

Treatment Services Offered Trend (2016-2020)

Psychotherapy and Behavioral Therapy remained the most commonly offered mental health services, showing consistent growth from 2016 to 2020. This stability underscores their foundational role in mental health care, as they are well-understood, evidence-based, and widely accepted by both patients and healthcare providers.

The number of facilities offering Advanced Therapies more than tripled during this period, reflecting a shift toward specialized, technology-driven treatment options. This trend may have been driven by advancements in technology, increased funding for mental health services, and the COVID-19 pandemic, which highlighted mental health challenges and created urgent demand for innovative solutions.
Dual Therapy and Cognitive Therapy also saw notable growth, likely influenced by the same factors, including an expanded focus on integrative and personalized care.
Similarly, Trauma Therapy and Activity-Based Therapy experienced steady growth, signaling a growing recognition of the value of trauma-informed care and non-traditional interventions in improving mental health outcomes. These trends reflect the mental health sector's efforts to address diverse patient needs and explore new therapeutic modalities.

Co-occurance of Treatment Offerings

Given the list of drugs offered at each facility, we grouped them by what they treat. Specifically, we have drugs for Schizophrenia Treatment, Bipolar Disorder Treatment, Psychotic Disorders (Non-Schizophrenic), Mood Stabilizers (Mania, Depression), Treatment-Resistant or Adjunctive Treatment, and Adjunctive Antidepressants. From the drug correlation heatmap, we see that even when other drugs are offered, Treatment-Resistant Treatments and Adjunctive Treatments are not typically offered. This is a huge problem as there are less treatment facilities out there that have the capabilities to support these deographics of people that need this treatment.

Treatments Offered By State

This problem is made more clear when we break it down to the specific drug, and for each state. Notice how in every state, the range of facilities that offer the drugs for each treatment types remains the same (in alaska the range for each drug type is about 4-25). So the reason that the co-occurrence heatmap showed less options for Treatment-Resistant Treatments and Adjunctive Antidepressant Treatments is that there are simply less treatment options for these categories than the others. For instance, there are 16 different types of treatment options for bipolar disorder and there are only 4 types of Treatment-Resistant Treatments. Adjunctive Antidepressants and Treatment-Resistant Treatments are typically used for those who do not repond adequately to standard treatment. For these individuals whose circumstances are already unique, we must continue to research to provide them with more treatment options and alternatives in the future.

Conclusion

The findings from this comprehensive analysis of mental health care facilities across the United States highlight several critical insights into access, distribution, therapy offerings, and treatment availability. The conclusions can be summarized as follows:

1. Core Therapies: Psychotherapy, Behavioral Therapy, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, and Group Therapy are widely available, underscoring their foundational role in mental health care.

2. Growth in Specialized Treatments: Advanced therapies, trauma-informed care, and activity-based interventions have significantly expanded, reflecting technological advancements and rising demand.

3. Facility Distribution Disparities: Larger states like Texas and Florida have low facilities-per-capita ratios, while the Northeast and Midwest lead in accessibility. The South lags behind significantly.

4. Service Gaps in Facilities: Residential facilities lack diversity in therapies, particularly trauma therapy, telemedicine, and language services, limiting access for underserved populations.

5. Treatment Gaps for Complex Cases: Facilities offering treatment-resistant therapies and adjunctive antidepressants are scarce, leaving critical gaps for those needing specialized care.

6. Regional Specialization: The Northeast offers diverse treatment options, the Midwest emphasizes community-based care, while the South and West lack comprehensive services.

7. Technology and Accessibility: Telemedicine thrives in VA facilities but needs expansion across all facility types to enhance rural and underserved access.

Recommendations

* Address regional disparities by expanding facilities in underserved areas.

* Invest in specialized treatment options for complex cases.

* Diversify services in residential facilities to improve inclusivity.

* Expand telemedicine and ensure equitable funding for public and nonprofit facilities.

By addressing these gaps and leveraging the insights gained from this analysis, the United States can work towards a more equitable and comprehensive mental health care system, ensuring that individuals across all states and demographics have access to the care they need.
