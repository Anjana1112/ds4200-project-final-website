Disparities in Mental Health Care Facilities

Introduction to Topic

Our project focuses on analyzing mental health service availability and disparities across the United States using data from the National Mental Health Services Survey (N-MHSS). This topic is critical as mental health care access remains inconsistent across different regions, demographic groups, and facility types, often leaving vulnerable populations underserved. By exploring factors such as geographic location, facility type, patient demographics, and the use of telemedicine, we aim to uncover patterns and disparities in mental health service provision. Understanding these trends is vital for policymakers and health care providers to address gaps in service, improve care equity, and ensure that individuals with mental health needs receive appropriate and timely treatment.

Introduction to Data

The data being used is from the National Mental Health Services Survey, which consolidates data from every known mental health treatment facility in the United States from 2010 to 2020. The types of mental health treatment facilities included in the dataset are as follows: psychiatric hospitals, general hospitals, Veterans Affairs medical centers, partial hospitalization/day treatment mental health facilities, outpatient mental health facilities, residential treatment centers for children, residential treatment centers for adults, multi-setting mental health facilities, community mental health centers, non-psychiatric hospitals, and other (any facilities that don’t fall into the previously named categories). The topics included in the survey include facility type, operation, and primary treatment focus, facility treatment characteristics (e.g., settings of care; mental health treatment modalities, pharmacotherapies; supportive services and practices; services provided to clients with co occurring mental health and substance use disorders; dedicated or exclusively designed programs or groups offered; seclusion or restraint practices; and crisis intervention team availability), facility operating characteristics (e.g., age groups accepted; availability of treatment services provided in non-English languages and in sign language for the deaf and hard-of-hearing; and smoking policy), facility management characteristics (e.g., quality improvement practices; use of electronic resources to accomplish work activities; licensure, certification, and accreditation; sources of payment and funding), and client characteristics. The data was collected through secure web-based questionnaires, paper questionnaires sent by mail, and computer-assisted telephone interviews. In 2020, 14,187 total eligible facilities were surveyed. The dataset is accessible in formats for SAS, SPSS, Stata, Delimited, and R, and can be downloaded through the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) website.

Data Source: National Mental Health Services Survey (N-MHSS)

Research Question

How do differences in facility distribution, therapy offerings, treatment availability, and facility types reflect the current state of access to mental health care in the United States?

Number of Facilities per 100k Across the US

This map is designed to show a comprehensive view of mental health facility distribution across the United States, balancing both numbers of facilities per 100,000 people. Here are the key takeaways:

Map: The map uses opacity and color intensity to represent the number of facilities per 100,000 people, not just the raw count of facilities. States with high populations, like California (CA) and Texas (TX), may appear lighter in color despite having a high number of facilities because the population size reduces the facilities-per-100k ratio. For instance, California has 970 facilities, but a facilities-per-100k value of 2.45, resulting in a lighter shade. Texas has 363 facilities, but with its large population, the facilities-per-100k ratio is only 1.25, making it even lighter in color.

High-Intensity States: States with smaller populations often have higher facilities per capita, leading to darker shades. Examples include Maine (ME) with 187 facilities but a facilities-per-100k ratio of 13.73, and Vermont (VT) with 60 facilities but a facilities-per-100k ratio of 9.33. These states illustrate how small populations can result in a high facilities-per-100k ratio even with fewer absolute facilities.

Interactive Features: The map is interactive and allows users to hover over each state to access key details, including State Name, the total population of the state, the number of facilities in the state, and Facilities per 100,000 People (the population-adjusted facility count).

Facility Types and Bar Charts: Clicking on a state marker reveals a horizontal bar chart that shows the distribution of facility types in that state. This gives insight into the types of mental health services offered, such as Inpatient Psychiatric Facilities, Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities, and Community-Based Mental Health Centers. States with larger populations, such as California (CA) and New York (NY), show a diverse mix dominated by Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities and Residential Treatment Facilities. The most common facility types across states include Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities, Community-Based Mental Health Centers, and Residential Treatment Facilities, while smaller categories like Veterans Administration Medical Centers (VAMC) and Multi-setting Mental Health Facilities are consistently less represented. This data provides critical insights into regional disparities and service availability, underscoring opportunities for targeted resource allocation.

Insights on Resource Allocation: This visualization highlights disparities in mental health facility access. Urbanized states may have high total counts but lower availability per capita, while less populated states may have fewer total facilities but higher per-capita access. Policymakers can use this map to identify areas where facilities need to be expanded to address gaps in availability, particularly in states like Texas (TX) and Florida (FL), where the facilities-per-100k values are low relative to their populations.

Underserved Regions: States like Texas (TX) (1.25 facilities per 100k) and Florida (FL) (2.29 facilities per 100k) have both high populations and relatively low facilities-per-100k ratios, indicating potential areas for improvement. Other states like Nevada (NV) and South Carolina (SC) also fall into lower tiers of facilities-per-100k.

Facility Types by Region

This stacked bar chart aggregates the data from individual states into regions of the United States, providing a clearer comparison of facility distribution on a regional level.

Regional Differences in Facilities Per Capita: The Northeast has the highest overall number of facilities per 100,000 people, with significant contributions from Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities and Community-Based Mental Health Centers. The Midwest follows, with a balanced distribution, where Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities and Community-Based Mental Health Centers make up the majority. The West has fewer facilities per capita overall, with a large portion attributed to Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities. The South has the lowest number of facilities per capita, with a smaller proportion of Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities and fewer contributions from other facility types.

Dominance of Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities: Across all regions, Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities are the largest contributors to the total facilities per capita, emphasizing their importance in mental health service delivery.

Regional Specialization: The Northeast has a relatively higher proportion of Inpatient Psychiatric Facilities and Multi-setting Mental Health Facilities, indicating a focus on diverse treatment options. The Midwest stands out for its emphasis on Community-Based Mental Health Centers compared to other regions.

Veterans Administration Medical Centers (VAMC): These facilities make up a very small proportion across all regions, suggesting either limited availability or lower population-adjusted needs.

Equity Implications: The South shows the lowest facilities per capita across almost all categories, potentially reflecting disparities in mental health service availability that might need to be addressed.

Therapy Services vs Facility Types

This radar chart compares the availability of various therapy services across different facility types. The key takeaways from this visualization are:

Telemedicine: Telemedicine services are highly available at Veterans Administration facilities, which is logical given that many veterans do not reside in these facilities but instead rely on remote access to support when needed. This highlights the facility’s adaptability to meet veterans’ needs through virtual care options.

Language Services: Inpatient facilities and community-based centers excel in providing language services, reflecting a strong commitment to inclusivity by offering care in languages other than English. Unfortunately, residential facilities fall significantly behind in this area, suggesting a lack of accessibility for non-English-speaking individuals in long-term care settings.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Group Therapy: These therapy types are among the most widely available services across all facility types, with their lines consistently near the outer edge of the chart. This indicates their role as foundational therapeutic approaches in mental health treatment.

Trauma Therapy: While trauma therapy is broadly available across most facility types, its provision is notably limited in residential and inpatient facilities. This raises potential concerns, as individuals in these settings may benefit significantly from trauma-informed care.

Activity Therapy: Residential and inpatient facilities are leaders in offering activity therapy, with inpatient facilities providing the highest levels. This emphasis aligns with the nature of these settings, where individuals, often residing for extended periods, have greater opportunities to engage in structured activities. In contrast, activity therapy availability in most other facilities is relatively low, often below 15%, except for multi-setting facilities.

Overall Trends: The chart underscores the strong presence of core therapies, such as cognitive behavioral and group therapies, across most facility types, while highlighting service gaps in areas like telemedicine, trauma therapy, and language services in specific settings. Residential facilities, in particular, appear to struggle with offering diverse therapy services, especially in language, telemedicine, and trauma therapy.

Treatment Services Offered Trend (2016-2020)

Psychotherapy and Behavioral Therapy remained the most commonly offered mental health services, showing growth from 2016 to 2020, with dips in between which may be due to funding issues or shift in focus to other resources. Being offered by the most number of facilities underscores their foundational role in mental health care, as they are well-understood, evidence-based, and widely accepted by both patients and healthcare providers.

The number of facilities offering Advanced Therapies more than tripled during this period, reflecting a shift toward specialized, technology-driven treatment options. This trend may have been driven by advancements in technology, increased funding for mental health services, and the COVID-19 pandemic, which created urgent demand for innovative solutions.
Dual Therapy and Cognitive Therapy also saw notable growth, likely influenced by the same factors, including an expanded focus on integrative and personalized care.
Similarly, Trauma and Activity-Based Therapies experienced steady growth, signaling a growing recognition of the value of trauma-informed care and non-traditional interventions in improving mental health outcomes.

Summary and additional work

The findings from this comprehensive analysis of mental health care facilities across the United States highlight several critical insights into access, distribution, therapy offerings, and treatment availability. The conclusions can be summarized as follows:

1. Core Therapies: Psychotherapy, Behavioral Therapy, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, and Group Therapy are widely available, underscoring their foundational role in mental health care.

2. Growth in Specialized Treatments: Advanced therapies, trauma-informed care, and activity-based interventions have significantly expanded, reflecting technological advancements and rising demand.

3. Facility Distribution Disparities: Larger states like Texas and Florida have low facilities-per-capita ratios, while the Northeast and Midwest lead in accessibility. The South lags behind significantly.

4. Service Gaps in Facilities: Residential facilities lack diversity in therapies, particularly trauma therapy, telemedicine, and language services, limiting access for underserved populations.

5. Treatment Gaps for Complex Cases: Facilities offering treatment-resistant therapies and adjunctive antidepressants are scarce, leaving critical gaps for those needing specialized care.

6. Regional Specialization: The Northeast offers diverse treatment options, the Midwest emphasizes community-based care, while the South and West lack comprehensive services.

7. Technology and Accessibility: Telemedicine thrives in VA facilities but needs expansion across all facility types to enhance rural and underserved access.


Recommendations

* Impact of Workforce Shortages: Analyze the distribution of qualified mental health professionals, especially in regions with low facility-per-capita ratios.

* Evaluate Facility Outcomes: Assess the success rates of therapies offered at facilities in different regions; identify correlations between facility density and mental health outcomes, such as reduced hospitalizations or improved quality of life.

*Funding Gaps and Policy Effectiveness: Examine state and federal funding allocation for mental health services and its impact on facility performance and accessibility.
