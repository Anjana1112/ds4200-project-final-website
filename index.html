Mental Health Care Disparities

Introduction to Topic

Our project focuses on analyzing mental health service availability and disparities across the United States using data from the National Mental Health Services Survey (N-MHSS). This topic is critical as mental health care access remains inconsistent across different regions, demographic groups, and facility types, often leaving vulnerable populations underserved. By exploring factors such as geographic location, facility type, patient demographics, and the use of telemedicine, we aim to uncover patterns and disparities in mental health service provision. Understanding these trends is vital for policymakers and health care providers to address gaps in service, improve care equity, and ensure that individuals with mental health needs receive appropriate and timely treatment.

References

Gao et al. (2024) highlight a global mental health treatment gap, with only 42-44% seeking care, especially in low- and middle-income nations. Access is influenced by factors like age, gender, ethnicity, and income, while barriers include cost, transportation, and stigma. Cantor et al. (2024) found that 80% of U.S. mental health facilities offer telehealth, with services varying by facility type and geography. Urban and private hospitals are more likely to provide telehealth, but access is uneven, with median wait times of 14 days. Mirza et al. (2020) explored language barriers in mental health care for patients with limited English proficiency, finding challenges in communication and a lack of qualified interpreters. The study emphasizes the need for tailored training to improve care. These papers inform our research on regional disparities, demographic factors, and the growing role of telemedicine, providing essential context for visualizing inequalities in mental health services.

Introduction to Data

The data being used is from the National Mental Health Services Survey, which consolidates data from every known mental health treatment facility in the United States from 2010 to 2020. The types of mental health treatment facilities included in the dataset are as follows: psychiatric hospitals, general hospitals, Veterans Affairs medical centers, partial hospitalization/day treatment mental health facilities, outpatient mental health facilities, residential treatment centers for children, residential treatment centers for adults, multi-setting mental health facilities, community mental health centers, non-psychiatric hospitals, and other (any facilities that donâ€™t fall into the previously named categories). The topics included in the survey include facility type, operation, and primary treatment focus, facility treatment characteristics (e.g., settings of care; mental health treatment modalities, pharmacotherapies; supportive services and practices; services provided to clients with co occurring mental health and substance use disorders; dedicated or exclusively designed programs or groups offered; seclusion or restraint practices; and crisis intervention team availability), facility operating characteristics (e.g., age groups accepted; availability of treatment services provided in non-English languages and in sign language for the deaf and hard-of-hearing; and smoking policy), facility management characteristics (e.g., quality improvement practices; use of electronic resources to accomplish work activities; licensure, certification, and accreditation; sources of payment and funding), and client characteristics. The data was collected through secure web-based questionnaires, paper questionnaires sent by mail, and computer-assisted telephone interviews. In 2020, 14,187 total eligible facilities were surveyed. The dataset is accessible in formats for SAS, SPSS, Stata, Delimited, and R, and can be downloaded through the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) website.

Mental Health Treatment Services Offered Trend (2016-2020)

Psychotherapy and Behavioral Therapy remained the most commonly offered services, with consistent growth across the years. Psychotherapy increased slightly from 2016 to 2020, while Behavioral Therapy peaked in 2019. These services' stability suggests they are foundational in mental health care, being well-understood and widely accepted by both patients and healthcare providers.

The number of facilities offering Advanced Therapies more than tripled from 2016 to 2020, suggesting a shift towards more specialized and innovative technology-driven treatment options, fueled by technological advancements, and possibly, the pandemic, which highlighted mental health challenges and led to an expansion in available mental health services, possibly due to increased funding, innovation, or urgent demand.
Dual and Cognitive Therapy saw notable growth at the same time which may also be attributed to the above reasons.

SLIDE 7

This map is designed to show a comprehensive view of mental health facility distribution across the United States, balancing both numbers of facilities per 100,000 people. Here are the key takeaways:

  1. Map:
    • The map uses opacity and color intensity to represent the number of facilities per 100,000 people, not just the raw count of facilities.
    • States with high populations, like California (CA) and Texas (TX), may appear lighter in color despite having a high number of facilities because the population size reduces the facilities-per-100k ratio.
      • For instance, California has 970 facilities, but a facilities-per-100k value of 2.45, resulting in a lighter shade.
      • Texas has 363 facilities, but with its large population, the facilities-per-100k ratio is only 1.25, making it even lighter in color.
  2. High-Intensity States:
    • States with smaller populations often have higher facilities per capita, leading to darker shades.
    • Examples include:
      • Maine (ME): 187 facilities, but with a facilities-per-100k ratio of 13.73.
      • Vermont (VT): 60 facilities, but a facilities-per-100k ratio of 9.33.
    • These states illustrate how small populations can result in a high facilities-per-100k ratio even with fewer absolute facilities.
  3. Interactive Features:
    • The map is interactive and allows users to hover over each state to access key details:
      • State Name
      • The total population of the state.
      • The number of facilities in the state.
      • Facilities per 100,000 People: The population-adjusted facility count.
  4. Facility Types and Pie Charts:
    • Clicking on a state marker reveals a pie chart that shows the distribution of facility types in that state.
    • This gives insight into the types of mental health services offered, such as:
      • Inpatient Psychiatric Facilities
      • Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities
      • Community-Based Mental Health Centers
    • States with larger populations, such as California (CA) and New York (NY) show a diverse mix dominated by Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities and Residential Treatment Facilities. The most common facility types across states include Outpatient/Day Treatment Facilities, Community-Based Mental Health Centers, and Residential Treatment Facilities, while smaller categories like Veterans Administration Medical Centers (VAMC) and Multi-setting Mental Health Facilities are consistently less represented. This data provides critical insights into regional disparities and service availability, underscoring opportunities for targeted resource allocation.
  5. Insights on Resource Allocation:
    • This visualization highlights disparities in mental health facility access:
    • Urbanized states may have high total counts but lower availability per capita.
    • Less populated states may have fewer total facilities but higher per-capita access.
    • Policymakers can use this map to identify areas where facilities need to be expanded to address gaps in availability, particularly in states like Texas (TX) and Florida (FL) where the facilities-per-100k values are low relative to their populations.
  6. Underserved Regions:
    • States like Texas (TX) (1.25 facilities per 100k) and Florida (FL) (2.29 facilities per 100k) have both high populations and relatively low facilities-per-100k ratios, indicating potential areas for improvement.
    • Other states like Nevada (NV) and South Carolina (SC) also fall into lower tiers of facilities-per-100k.
